How this should feel

Like a field journal you actually want to read again. Every plant page can carry multiple dated entries over time, while full-bed pages hold design ideas, experiments, and changes worth remembering.

Springbud break, bloom overlap, first flushes
Summerheat, fullness, flowers, stress and recovery
Fallcolor shift, seedheads, structure, lessons
Winterframework, pruning notes, plans for next year

Plant timelines

One page per plant, many entries over time. Best for maples, hydrangeas, shrubs, vines, and fruit trees.

Bed journals

Use full-bed pages for design records, before-and-after ideas, and places where multiple plants matter together.

Editorial over clutter

The good photos get stronger writing. Repeat shots go into existing timelines instead of becoming random one-off pages.

The page types that make sense

Plant pages

For plants worth following over time. Add new dated entries to the same page so the year builds naturally.

Planning pages

For undecided spots, layout changes, bed renovations, and planting ideas that need a visual record.

Seasonal archive later

Once the volume grows, add seasonal index pages so Spring, Summer, and Fall can each have their own memory lane.

Calendar stays practical

The Garden Calendar remains the working tool, while Plant Progress becomes the more beautiful record of what actually happened.

The goal is not to prove every photo exists. It’s to make the garden feel legible, memorable, and worth revisiting when the season has moved on.
